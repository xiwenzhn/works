Sergei Eisenstein theorized dialectical montage, emphasizing the eye's role in synthesizing images into motion through “eye-memory,” which processes cinema's temporal flow. Paul Virilio extends this by noting the eye's incessant movements, composing vision from fragments. By mimicking this temporal perception, the following film relies on the eye to register the defile of the image as motion in time. Digital technologies disrupt this by operating at electromagnetic speeds, bypassing the eye’s natural processing. The film, envisioned as part of a duo-channel installation, continually contrasts analog imagery with digital pixelation. Superimposed films obscure the spectator, destabilizing the gaze and critiquing vision’s waning authority. The subjective camera and unstable eye conjure Virilio’s remark: “no continuous consciousness…only compositions of consciousness.”
The film work explores the tension between analog and digital media. Experiments with motion and perception. Visual studies in time and space. Interactive digital experiences. Cinematic explorations of movement.
